Research Title

Enhancing Meditation Through Virtual Reality: The Effect of VR on Mindfulness and Well-Being

Abstract

In the evolving nexus of ancient traditions and technological progress, the fusion of millennia-old meditation practices with Virtual Reality (VR) unfolds a captivating frontier for well-being. Using VR in the realm of meditation is becoming more and more popular with an increasing number of VR meditation applications being developed. However, relevant research on the effectiveness and advantages of VR meditation over traditional audio-guided meditation alone is scarce and inconclusive. Our research, following a preliminary study design, aims at comparing VR meditation to traditional audio practices in the dimensions of concentration, temporal perception, and stress reduction. Additionally, we explore the possibility and benefits of group meditation sessions in VR against single-user experiences. Our study will follow a between-groups design where participants will be immersed in various VR meditative settings accompanied by audio instructions. Some participants will experience the session in an alone setting, whereas others will take part in a group-setting where they will be joined by other virtual characters (avatars). A control group will listen to the same audio medication but without being immersed in the VR setting. Participants in all groups will complete both subjective and objective reports and tests before and after the experience. These will include self-reported levels of stress and concentrations, a Stroop test to assess concentration, and a measurement of the perceived duration of the experience. The VR experience itself will be evaluated based on standardized VR questionnaires that assess the levels of immersion, presence, and cybersickness. Before starting the meditation, participants will have to fill out a pre-experience questionnaire, designed to collect some background information and other demographic data and to obtain their self-reported stress levels using a 0 (not at all) to 10 (very much) Likert scale; then they will complete a standardized computerized version of the Stroop task. The millisecond unit Stroop effect value will be automatically generated by the computer and is used to evaluate participants’ concentration levels by comparing their reaction speeds in instances where the word and its color match versus where they do not. During the meditation, participants will be prompted through audio guidance to rate their own stress and concentration levels at various moments, which will help us assess their adaptability to the VR environment and the guided audio. We will compare the results of the audio-only meditations and the VR meditation to identify any potential differences. Our study has the potential to offer preliminary insights into how VR can be applied to meditation and provide valuable guidance for both VR developers and users. It presents an alternative perspective for individuals interested in pursuing VR meditation instead of audio-only meditation.

Implementation

As the team leader, I designed and implemented the VR scenes for both inddividual and group meditation sessions as well as participant survey within the VR scene in Unity3D. Some key aspects the implementation include immersiveness and embodiement, which determine the success of the VR environment and reliability of the result. To achieve a high level immersiveness and comfortable, authentic embodiment, I carefully designed both the surroundings and avatars. In the individual session, the spatial relation between the surrounding objects and the embodied avatar is very important. It could not be so overwhelming that the participant would feel stressed, nor could it be so distant that the participant would feel disconnected from the environment. Threfore, I deliberately removed some of the objects, such as grass, trees, and flowers from the surrounding of the avatar to keep the avatar some distance from the environment but still possess a sense of presence in the virtual environment. As for the group sessions, the presence of other animated avatars is also critical as it has to be natural and authentic in order for the participant to feel comfortable to experience meditation sessions with them. It is important not to make the presence of other avatars a distracting factor for the participant. Thus, I added the self-introduction part of the animated avatars with lip-sync as they speak to enhance their credibility to the participant. It is also expected that the participant would feel more comfortable to haven the animated avatars in the group meditation session.

Both the VR and lip-sync functions are based on the Unity packages introduced by the Instructor and the animations of the avatars in group sessions are realized through the built-in animation module of Unity3D.

Result and Discussion

This study finds varied results regarding various parameters in measuring the effectiveness of VR in meditation. Please refer to the Poster image for detailed results and analysis.

This study on VR meditation was constrained by the number of participants and the implementation of the VR experience. Additionally, the duration of the meditation sessions may have impacted the results. For some participants, particularly in the VR environment, the length of meditation might have been too long, potentially affecting their concentration and stress levels. Therefore, future research should consider a larger sample size, improved VR designs, varied meditation durations consulted with professional therapists or psychological experts, and more comprehensive data collection and analysis.
